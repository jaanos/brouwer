Fingerprints of Projective Planes of Order 25

Here are the fingerprints of the 193 projective planes of order 25 which are known to me. This isomorphism invariant succeeds in distinguishing all non-isomorphic pairs among the known planes of order 25, except for the Foulser planes b1 and b2 (and their duals b1Dual and b2Dual). These pairs of planes are, however, distinguished by other invariants, such as the 5-rank.

Definition of Fingerprint

Given a projective plane of order n (which has N=n2+n+1 points and N lines), consider the N-by-N matrix A with entries aP,L defined as follows. For each point P, choose an arbitrary but fixed ordering of the lines through P as 1st, 2nd, 3rd, ..., (n+1)st. For each line L, choose an arbitrary but fixed ordering of the points on L as 1st, 2nd, 3rd, ..., (n+1)st. Now for each non-incident point-line pair (P,L), the incidence relation naturally provides a bijection between the n+1 lines through P and the n+1 points on L. Relative to the chosen orderings, this gives a permutation of degree n+1. Define aP,L to be the sign (1 or −1) of this permutation. If P lies on L, define aP,L=0.

Note that different choices of orderings for the points and lines have the effect of possibly permuting the rows and columns of A, and possibly multiplying certain rows and columns by −1. Therefore the multiset consisting of the absolute values of the entries of the matrix B=AAT is an isomorphism invariant of the projective plane. This multiset (i.e. set with multiplicities attached to its elements), due to J.H. Conway, is known as the (complete) fingerprint of the plane.

For translation planes one obtains a computationally cheaper isomorphism invariant, called the (restricted) fingerprint, by replacing A by the (N+1)-by-(N+1) submatrix thereof having rows indexed by the points on the translation line, and columns indexed by lines through the origin. In the case of translation planes of order q2 with kernel containing GF(q), this restricted fingerprint is more readily computed directly from the corresponding ovoid of the Klein quadric, as shown by G.E. Moorhouse, Two-graphs and skew two-graphs in finite geometries, Linear Algebra and its Applications 226-228 (1995), 529-551.

Since I am comparing more planes than just translation planes, I have listed only the complete fingerprints. The restricted fingerprints of the translation planes of order 25 have been published by C. Charnes, Quadratic matrices and the translation planes of order 52, in Coding Theory, Design Theory, Group Theory (Proceedings of the Marshall Hall Conference), ed. D. Jungnickel and S. A. Vanstone, J. Wiley & Sons, 1993, pp.155-161. Note however that Charnes there uses a slightly different nomenclature for the 21 translation planes than that which appears in the final corrected and published version of the paper of Czerwinski and Oakden.
